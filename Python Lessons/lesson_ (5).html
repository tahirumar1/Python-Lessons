What is Mad Libs?

Mad Libs is a word-substitution game: a story has blank spaces labelled by word type (noun, verb, adjective…). Players fill in words without seeing the story. The result is a funny, often nonsensical tale. It is a perfect first project because the Python skills map directly: input() collects the words, variables store them, and f-strings slot them into the story. Students produce something they can immediately share with friends and family.

🎯The goal of a project day: Students leave with a complete, working program they built themselves — not a tutorial they followed. Resist the urge to live-code the solution. Circulate, ask questions, let them struggle productively.
Learning objectives
  • Apply print(), variables, input(), f-strings, and arithmetic in one complete program
  • Write a program from scratch without a starter template
  • Debug their own code independently using error messages
  • Read and explain someone else's code during peer review
  • Present their work and describe at least one design decision they made

Students plan their story (5 min)
Students write their story outline on paper first — not in Python. They decide: the theme, 5 blank slots, and what word type each blank needs (noun, adjective, verb, number, name…). Planning on paper prevents blank-editor paralysis. Teacher circulates and approves plans before students open their editor.

Students build their Mad Libs program. Requirements checklist below. Teacher circulates silently for the first 5 minutes — let students attempt on their own before offering help. When helping: ask guiding questions rather than giving code. "What does input() return?" "How do you put a variable into a string?"

Pairs swap computers. Each student runs their partner's program, fills in silly words, and writes feedback on the peer review card below. They must find one thing that works well and one thing to improve. No verbal feedback yet — written only so everyone gets a response.

✓

✓

✓

✓

✓

✓

Extension challenges — for fast finishers
⭐ Level 1
Word count
Count the total characters in all the words combined using len() and print it at the end of the story.
⭐⭐ Level 2
Two stories
Collect words once and use them in two different story templates — so the same inputs produce two different tales.
⭐⭐⭐ Level 3
Score the silliness
Ask the user to rate how silly the story is (1–10). Use % and // to compute a "silliness percentage" and print it with a :.1f format.

Teacher example — Space Adventure Mad Libs
space_madlibs.py — teacher solution
# ============================================
#  SPACE ADVENTURE MAD LIBS
# ============================================

print("🚀 SPACE ADVENTURE MAD LIBS 🚀")
print("Fill in the blanks to create your story!")
print("=" * 40)
print()

# Collect words from the user
astronaut_name = input("Enter an astronaut's name: ")
planet         = input("Enter a planet name: ")
adjective      = input("Enter an adjective: ")
alien_food     = input("Enter a type of food: ")
verb_ing       = input("Enter a verb ending in -ing: ")
num_days       = int(input("Enter a number of days: "))
speed_kmh      = float(input("Enter a speed in km/h: "))

# Arithmetic
total_hours  = num_days * 24
distance_km  = speed_kmh * total_hours

# Print the story
print()
print("=" * 40)
print("       YOUR SPACE STORY")
print("=" * 40)
print()
print(f"Astronaut {astronaut_name} blasted off toward the {adjective}")
print(f"planet of {planet}, travelling at {speed_kmh:.1f} km/h.")
print()
print(f"After {num_days} days ({total_hours} hours) of {verb_ing} through")
print(f"deep space, they had covered {distance_km:,.0f} kilometres.")
print()
print(f"The aliens greeted them with a feast of {alien_food}.")
print(f'{astronaut_name} said: "I have travelled far for this {alien_food}."')
print()
print("=" * 40)
print(f"Story stats: {len(astronaut_name)}-letter astronaut · {num_days} days · {distance_km:,.0f} km")

Teacher notes
⚠️Biggest trap: Students who spend 10 minutes staring at a blank screen. The planning-on-paper step prevents this — enforce it. A student who has a story written down will always start faster than one who doesn't.
⚠️Common bug: Students whose story has a quote character inside an f-string — e.g. f"She said "hello"" — causing a SyntaxError. Fix: use single quotes for the outer string: f'She said "hello"'. The teacher example shows this technique on the last story line.
💡Pacing: 25 minutes is tight for some students. If a student hasn't finished by the peer review phase, pair them with a fast finisher who can help them complete it during the review window. Never skip the showcase — it is the motivational payoff for the whole week.
💡The "="*40 trick: Students will notice the banner lines in the teacher example use "=" * 40. This string repetition operator wasn't explicitly taught — it's a natural discovery moment. Let students ask about it. It previews loops beautifully.
❤️Celebration: End the lesson by having the class applaud every presenter. Week 1 is complete. Students who had never written a line of code on Monday have a working interactive program by Friday. Make sure they feel that.
